# Product Management Scoring Criteria

## The Core Question
"Can this person own a product area, make good prioritization decisions, and ship outcomes through cross-functional teams?"

## Scored Dimensions (5)

### 1. Product Ownership and Shipping (Weight: 25%)
Did they own a product or feature area end-to-end? Did things ship because of their decisions?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Owned a product area with clear outcomes. Defined roadmap, made scope decisions, shipped to users. Has metrics showing impact. |
| Solid (60-79) | Contributed to product decisions but shared ownership. Or owned a smaller scope (single feature vs product area). |
| Partial (40-59) | Was involved in shipping but in a supporting role (BA, project manager, scrum master). Made recommendations but didn't own decisions. |
| Weak (20-39) | Worked adjacent to product teams but didn't participate in product decisions directly. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of product ownership or shipping in any capacity. |

### 2. Stakeholder Alignment and Influence (Weight: 20%)
Can they get alignment across teams without direct authority? This is the PM superpower.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Led cross-functional alignment across 3+ teams. Resolved conflicts. Made decisions stick without positional authority. Specific examples. |
| Solid (60-79) | Coordinated across 2+ teams regularly. Some evidence of influencing decisions. |
| Partial (40-59) | Worked cross-functionally but more in a coordination role than an influence role. |
| Weak (20-39) | Mostly worked within a single team or function. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of cross-functional work. |

### 3. User and Market Insight (Weight: 20%)
Do they make decisions based on user understanding, not just stakeholder requests?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Conducted user research (interviews, surveys, usability tests). Decisions traced to user insights. Market or competitive analysis informed strategy. |
| Solid (60-79) | Gathered requirements from users/stakeholders and translated into product decisions. Some evidence of user-driven thinking. |
| Partial (40-59) | Worked with user feedback but didn't drive research. Reactive to requests rather than proactive discovery. |
| Weak (20-39) | Built what was asked for without questioning whether it was the right thing. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of user-facing work or market awareness. |

### 4. Strategic and Analytical Thinking (Weight: 20%)
Can they frame problems, define success metrics, and make tradeoff decisions with incomplete information?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Defined product strategy or vision. Set and tracked metrics. Made tradeoff decisions and can articulate the reasoning. Evidence of saying "no" to things. |
| Solid (60-79) | Contributed to strategy. Tracked metrics. Some evidence of tradeoff thinking. |
| Partial (40-59) | Executed strategy set by others. Worked with metrics but didn't define them. |
| Weak (20-39) | Focused on execution without strategic context. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of strategic thinking or metrics-driven work. |

### 5. Technical Fluency (Weight: 15%)
Can they work effectively with engineering teams? They don't need to code, but they need to speak the language.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Technical background (CS degree, engineering experience) or deep evidence of working with complex technical systems. Can discuss architecture tradeoffs. |
| Solid (60-79) | Works comfortably with engineers. Understands system constraints. Has worked on technically complex products. |
| Partial (40-59) | Works with technical teams but relies on others for technical judgment. |
| Weak (20-39) | Limited technical exposure. Mostly worked on business-facing products with simple tech. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of technical collaboration or understanding. |

## Seniority Calibration

The same dimensions apply at all levels, but the BAR shifts:

| Level | What Changes |
|---|---|
| Associate/Junior PM | Ownership at feature level. Execution focus. Learning to influence. |
| Mid PM | Ownership at product area level. Balanced strategy and execution. Can influence peers. |
| Senior PM | Ownership at product line level. Strategy-led. Influences leadership and cross-org. |
| Director+ | Ownership at portfolio level. Sets direction for multiple teams. Organizational influence. |

When scoring, check whether the candidate's shown level matches the role's seniority. A strong mid-level PM applying for a senior role might score 65-75 even with great evidence, because the scope gap matters.

## Common Scoring Traps

- Don't over-credit "years of experience." 8 years of the same work repeated is weaker than 4 years of progressive growth.
- Product Owners in Scrum teams sometimes did full PM work and sometimes just managed the backlog. Look at the actual work, not the title.
- Startup PMs often did everything (PM + design + marketing + ops). Give credit for the PM parts but don't assume depth in any one area.
- Enterprise PMs often had narrower scope but deeper stakeholder complexity. Don't penalize the narrower scope if the role calls for enterprise skills.

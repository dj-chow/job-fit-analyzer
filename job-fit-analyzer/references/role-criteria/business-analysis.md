# Business Analysis / BSA Scoring Criteria

Covers: Business Analyst, Business Systems Analyst (BSA), Senior BA, Lead BA, Requirements Analyst, Systems Analyst.

## The Core Question
"Can this person bridge business needs and technical solutions, translating ambiguous problems into clear, implementable requirements?"

## Scored Dimensions (5)

### 1. Requirements Engineering (Weight: 30%)
Can they take messy business needs and turn them into something a development team can build?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Wrote detailed functional and non-functional requirements. Created acceptance criteria that development teams could build against without constant clarification. Evidence of handling complex, multi-system requirements. Dealt with ambiguity and conflicting stakeholder needs. |
| Solid (60-79) | Writes clear requirements. Handles standard complexity well. Some experience with acceptance criteria and user stories. |
| Partial (40-59) | Gathers requirements but relies on others to refine them into buildable specs. Or writes requirements for simple, single-system features. |
| Weak (20-39) | Limited requirements experience. More focused on testing, reporting, or project support. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of requirements work. |

### 2. Systems and Data Understanding (Weight: 25%)
Do they understand how systems work well enough to ask the right questions and spot gaps?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Maps end-to-end workflows across multiple systems. Understands data flows, dependencies, and integration points. Uses SQL or other tools to validate data independently. Can trace an issue from business impact back to root cause in the system. |
| Solid (60-79) | Good understanding of the systems they work with. Can read data models and understand basic integrations. Does some data validation work. |
| Partial (40-59) | Works with systems at a surface level. Understands inputs and outputs but not the internals. Relies on technical teams for data questions. |
| Weak (20-39) | Limited systems knowledge. Primarily focused on business process rather than system behavior. |
| Gap (0-19) | No technical or systems exposure. |

### 3. Stakeholder Management and Communication (Weight: 20%)
Can they work the room? BAs live at the intersection of business and technology. Both sides need to trust them.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Runs working sessions with business and technical stakeholders. Translates between business language and technical language fluently. Manages conflicting priorities. Gets sign-off on difficult decisions. |
| Solid (60-79) | Communicates well with both business and technical teams. Can run requirements sessions. Handles standard stakeholder dynamics. |
| Partial (40-59) | Comfortable with one side (business or technical) but weaker with the other. Or works primarily with a single stakeholder group. |
| Weak (20-39) | Mostly takes direction rather than facilitating. Limited multi-stakeholder experience. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of stakeholder-facing work. |

### 4. Domain Expertise (Weight: 15%)
Do they know the industry well enough to ask smart questions and catch what others miss?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Deep domain knowledge in the relevant industry (finance, healthcare, insurance, etc.). Understands regulatory requirements, business processes, and industry-specific terminology. Can challenge business stakeholders from a place of knowledge. |
| Solid (60-79) | Good working knowledge of the domain. Familiar with key processes and terminology. |
| Partial (40-59) | General business knowledge that transfers. New to the specific domain but has learned adjacent ones before. |
| Weak (20-39) | No relevant domain experience. Would need significant ramp time. |
| Gap (0-19) | No business domain knowledge. |

Note: domain weight increases when the JD specifically calls out industry experience (e.g., "financial services experience required"). In those cases, increase to 25% and reduce Requirements Engineering to 20%.

### 5. Process and Methodology (Weight: 10%)
Do they understand how delivery works? Agile, waterfall, hybrid. Not just the ceremony names, but how to make the process actually work.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has worked in multiple delivery methodologies. Understands when to use what. Has improved team processes. Can manage backlogs, run refinement sessions, and coordinate across sprints or releases. |
| Solid (60-79) | Comfortable in agile or waterfall environments. Understands backlog management and sprint cadence. |
| Partial (40-59) | Has worked within a methodology but didn't drive it. Follows the process without shaping it. |
| Weak (20-39) | Limited awareness of delivery methodologies. |
| Gap (0-19) | No process experience. |

## Seniority Calibration

| Level | Scope Expectation |
|---|---|
| Junior BA (0-2 years) | Single feature or process. Needs guidance on requirements structure. Learning to ask good questions. |
| Mid BA (2-5 years) | Owns requirements for a workstream or project. Runs sessions independently. Handles moderate complexity. |
| Senior BA (5-8 years) | Owns requirements across multiple workstreams. Handles complex, multi-system problems. Mentors junior BAs. Trusted by both business and tech. |
| Lead BA (8+ years) | Shapes the BA practice. Handles the most complex, ambiguous problems. Influences delivery approach and team structure. Often plays a hybrid BA/PM role. |

## BA-to-PM Transition Signals

Many BA roles overlap heavily with PM work. Look for these signals that a BA was functioning as a de facto PM:

- Owned prioritization decisions (not just documenting what others decided)
- Defined product vision or strategy for their area
- Made tradeoff decisions between scope, timeline, and quality
- Owned the backlog with authority to say "no" to stakeholders
- Tracked outcomes post-launch, not just requirements pre-launch

If these signals are present, the candidate may score well against PM criteria too. Flag this in the Adjacent Work section.

## Common Scoring Traps

- Don't assume all BAs are the same. A BSA at a bank doing regulatory reporting lives in a completely different world than a BA at a startup writing user stories.
- "Requirements gathering" sounds passive. Look for evidence of requirements ENGINEERING, which means analysis, challenge, and synthesis, not just transcription.
- SQL skills are a differentiator. A BA who can independently validate data is far more effective than one who files tickets with the data team for every question.
- Certifications (CBAP, CCBA) are neutral signals. They don't hurt but they don't predict performance. Weight proven work over credentials.

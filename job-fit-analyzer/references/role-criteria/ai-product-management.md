# AI / ML Product Management Scoring Criteria

This role is not the same as normal PM. The bar and signals differ. Based on hiring data from OpenAI, Google, Amazon, Meta, and research across 18,000+ AI PM pros.

## The Core Question
"Can this person build AI products that work for real users, while dealing with the doubt that comes with systems that guess?"

## What Makes AI PM Different From Normal PM

- The core output shifts from PRDs to eval setups (evals).
- Results are based on odds, not sure things. You manage quality bars, not feature lists.
- You often start from a research skill looking for market fit, not from a user pain.
- Plans are loose. Hitting 60-70% of goals per quarter is normal.
- The team has researchers and devs. Day-one work with research is the norm.

## Scored Dimensions (6)

### 1. Shipped AI/ML Product Work (Weight: 25%)
This is the top signal. Did they build something with AI that real users took up?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Shipped an AI product to real users. Can point to use data. Dealt with model quality, edge cases, and user trust. |
| Solid (60-79) | Worked on an AI product in a support role. Or shipped a product that feeds ML systems, like a data platform. |
| Partial (40-59) | Built AI demos or early tests. Used AI tools in their PM work. Has a side project using AI. |
| Weak (20-39) | Took AI/ML courses. Knows the ideas but has not built. |
| Gap (0-19) | No proof of AI/ML product work or real AI contact. |

Note: a normal PM with strong skills but zero AI contact will score 0-19 here. That is a 25% weight at zero. It makes it very hard to score above 60 total. This reflects the market: AI PM roles strongly prefer people with hands-on AI product work.

### 2. Technical Fluency for AI (Weight: 20%)
Can they talk about model limits, data quality, evals, and training methods with care? Not coding, but strong tech grasp.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Can discuss recall vs. precision, training data needs, model eval rules, and deploy plans. Has worked with ML devs or researchers. |
| Solid (60-79) | Gets ML concepts well enough to have good talks with tech teams. Has worked on data-heavy products. |
| Partial (40-59) | General tech skills. Fine with APIs and data but little ML-specific grasp. |
| Weak (20-39) | Non-tech background. Learning AI ideas but can't yet go deep with ML teams. |
| Gap (0-19) | No tech fluency proof. |

### 3. Eval and Quality Thinking (Weight: 15%)
This is the new PM super skill in AI. Can they define what "good" looks like for a system that guesses?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has written evals, set quality bars, or built test setups for AI outputs. Gets MVQ (Minimum Viable Quality). |
| Solid (60-79) | Has set success metrics for fuzzy products. Has run A/B tests or done work that could transfer. |
| Partial (40-59) | Has tracked metrics but has not dealt with the blur of guess-based outputs. |
| Weak (20-39) | Metrics work is limited to ops or vanity metrics. |
| Gap (0-19) | No proof of quality framework thinking. |

### 4. Product Ownership and Shipping (Weight: 15%)
Same as normal PM but in any product setting. Did they own and ship?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Owned a product area end-to-end. Shipped to users. Clear results. |
| Solid (60-79) | Helped make product calls. Shipped features or products in a shared model. |
| Partial (40-59) | Support role in shipping. Gave input but did not own calls. |
| Weak (20-39) | Near product teams with no direct part. |
| Gap (0-19) | No shipping proof. |

### 5. Breadth of Domain Experience (Weight: 15%)
AI PM roles reward people who have worked across many problem spaces. Narrow experts are at a loss.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has worked across 3+ distinct domains (e.g., fraud + growth + enterprise tools). Brings many lenses to unclear problems. |
| Solid (60-79) | Has worked in 2 domains or one domain with varied problem types. |
| Partial (40-59) | Deep in one domain. Good skill but limited range. |
| Weak (20-39) | One domain, one product type for the whole career. |
| Gap (0-19) | Very early career or non-product background. |

### 6. Getting Teams Aligned (Weight: 10%)
Same as normal PM. Can they align cross-team work? Lower weight because AI PM roles often have smaller, more self-run teams.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Led cross-team work across tech and non-tech groups. Proof of shaping research goals or business plans. |
| Solid (60-79) | Worked across teams on a regular basis. |
| Partial (40-59) | More of a helper role than a driver role. |
| Weak (20-39) | Mostly worked in one team. |
| Gap (0-19) | No cross-team proof. |

## AI PM Archetype Detection

Two distinct types exist. The JD usually hints at which one:

**AI Builder PM**: Works with researchers to train, test, and improve models. Close to the ML pipeline. JD signals: "model work," "training data," "research," "model eval."

**AI Experience PM**: Turns model powers into user-facing products. Close to the user. JD signals: "user experience," "product design," "feature work," "use metrics."

Some roles blend both. When scoring, weight Technical Fluency more for Builder PMs and Product Ownership more for Experience PMs.

## Calibration Notes

- A normal PM with 10 years of work but zero AI contact will likely score 45-55. The AI-specific areas pull them down hard. This is honest. Market data backs it: "almost all AI PM jobs ask for prior hands-on AI PM work."
- A junior PM who has shipped one AI side project might score higher on the AI areas than a senior PM who has not. That is by design.
- Domain skill in regulated fields (banking, health, insurance) is a big bonus when the AI role involves rules or compliance. Add up to 5 points when the JD calls for it.
- Builder mindset (can prototype with AI tools like Cursor or Claude) is a good signal even if they have not shipped an AI product yet. Score it as Partial (40-59) on Dimension 1.

# AI / ML Product Management Scoring Criteria

This role is distinct from traditional PM. The evaluation bar and signals are different. Calibrated using hiring manager interviews from OpenAI, Google, Amazon, Meta, and industry research across 18,000+ AI PM professionals.

## The Core Question
"Can this person build AI-powered products that work for real users, while navigating the uncertainty that comes with probabilistic systems?"

## What Makes AI PM Different From Traditional PM

- The core artifact shifts from PRDs to evaluation frameworks (evals)
- Outcomes are probabilistic, not deterministic. You manage quality thresholds, not feature checklists.
- You often start from a research capability looking for product-market fit, not from a user problem
- Planning happens "in pencil, not pen." Completion rates of 60-70% per quarter are normal and expected.
- The team includes researchers alongside engineers. Day-one collaboration with research is standard.

## Scored Dimensions (6)

### 1. Shipped AI/ML Product Experience (Weight: 25%)
This is the single strongest signal. Did they build something with AI that real users adopted?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Shipped an AI-powered product to real users. Can point to adoption metrics. Dealt with model quality, edge cases, and user trust. |
| Solid (60-79) | Worked on an AI product in a contributing role. Or shipped an AI-adjacent product (data platform that feeds ML systems, for example). |
| Partial (40-59) | Built AI prototypes or proofs of concept. Used AI tools in their PM workflow. Has a side project using AI. |
| Weak (20-39) | Took AI/ML courses or certifications. Understands concepts but hasn't built. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of AI/ML product work or meaningful AI exposure. |

Note: a traditional PM with strong product skills but zero AI exposure will score 0-19 on this dimension. That's a 25% weight hitting zero, which makes it very hard to score above 60 overall. This reflects reality: AI PM roles strongly prefer candidates with hands-on AI product experience.

### 2. Technical Fluency for AI (Weight: 20%)
Can they discuss model constraints, data quality, evals, and training approaches with precision? Not coding, but "mastering technical influence."

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Can discuss precision/recall tradeoffs, training data needs, model evaluation criteria, and deployment strategies. Has worked directly with ML engineers or researchers. |
| Solid (60-79) | Understands ML concepts well enough to have productive conversations with technical teams. Has worked on data-intensive products. |
| Partial (40-59) | General technical literacy. Comfortable with APIs and data but limited ML-specific understanding. |
| Weak (20-39) | Non-technical background. Learning AI concepts but can't yet engage deeply with ML teams. |
| Gap (0-19) | No technical fluency evidence. |

### 3. Evaluation and Quality Framework Thinking (Weight: 15%)
This is the new PM superpower in AI. Can they define what "good" looks like for a probabilistic system?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has written evals, defined quality thresholds, or built testing frameworks for AI outputs. Understands MVQ (Minimum Viable Quality). |
| Solid (60-79) | Has defined success metrics for ambiguous products. Has experience with A/B testing or experimentation frameworks that could transfer. |
| Partial (40-59) | Has tracked product metrics but hasn't dealt with the ambiguity of probabilistic outputs. |
| Weak (20-39) | Metrics experience is limited to operational or vanity metrics. |
| Gap (0-19) | No evidence of quality framework thinking. |

### 4. Product Ownership and Shipping (Weight: 15%)
Same as traditional PM but applied to any product context. Did they own and ship?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Owned a product area end-to-end. Shipped to users. Clear outcomes. |
| Solid (60-79) | Contributed to product decisions. Shipped features or products in a shared ownership model. |
| Partial (40-59) | Supporting role in shipping. Made recommendations but didn't own decisions. |
| Weak (20-39) | Adjacent to product teams without direct involvement. |
| Gap (0-19) | No shipping evidence. |

### 5. Breadth of Domain Experience (Weight: 15%)
AI PM roles reward candidates who've worked across multiple problem spaces. Narrow specialists are at a disadvantage.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has worked across 3+ distinct product domains (e.g., fraud + growth + enterprise tools). Brings multiple lenses to ambiguous problems. |
| Solid (60-79) | Has worked in 2 distinct domains or one domain with varied problem types. |
| Partial (40-59) | Deep in one domain. Good expertise but limited range. |
| Weak (20-39) | Single domain, single product type throughout career. |
| Gap (0-19) | Very early career or non-product background. |

### 6. Stakeholder Alignment and Influence (Weight: 10%)
Same as traditional PM. Can they align cross-functional teams? Lower weight because AI PM roles often have smaller, more autonomous teams.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Led cross-functional alignment across technical and non-technical teams. Evidence of influencing research direction or business strategy. |
| Solid (60-79) | Coordinated across teams regularly. |
| Partial (40-59) | Coordination role rather than influence role. |
| Weak (20-39) | Mostly worked within a single team. |
| Gap (0-19) | No cross-functional evidence. |

## AI PM Archetype Detection

Two distinct types exist. The JD usually signals which one:

**AI Builder PM**: Works with researchers to train, test, and improve models. Closer to the ML pipeline. JD signals: "model development," "training data," "research collaboration," "model evaluation."

**AI Experience PM**: Transforms model capabilities into user-facing products. Closer to the user. JD signals: "user experience," "product design," "feature development," "adoption metrics."

Some roles blend both. When scoring, weight Technical Fluency higher for Builder PMs and Product Ownership higher for Experience PMs.

## Calibration Notes

- A traditional PM with 10 years of experience but zero AI exposure will likely score 45-55. The AI-specific dimensions pull them down hard. This is honest. The market data confirms: "almost all AI PM jobs ask for prior hands-on AI PM experience."
- A junior PM who has shipped one AI side project might score higher on the AI dimensions than a senior PM who hasn't. That's by design.
- Regulatory domain expertise (banking, healthcare, insurance) is a significant bonus when the AI role involves compliance or governance. Add up to 5 points as an override when the JD specifically calls for regulated industry experience.
- Builder mentality (can prototype with AI tools like Cursor or Claude) is a positive signal even if the candidate hasn't formally shipped an AI product. Score it as Partial (40-59) on Dimension 1.

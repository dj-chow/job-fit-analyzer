# Business Analysis / BSA Scoring Criteria

Covers: Business Analyst, Business Systems Analyst (BSA), Senior BA, Lead BA, Requirements Analyst, Systems Analyst.

## The Core Question
"Can this person bridge business needs and tech fixes, turning messy problems into clear specs a team can build?"

## Scored Dimensions (5)

### 1. Requirements Work (Weight: 30%)
Can they take messy business needs and turn them into something a dev team can build?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Wrote clear functional and non-functional specs. Made acceptance criteria that dev teams could build from with few questions. Proof of handling complex, multi-system needs. Dealt with vague asks and clashing goals. |
| Solid (60-79) | Writes clear specs. Handles normal depth well. Some work with acceptance criteria and user stories. |
| Partial (40-59) | Gathers needs but leans on others to refine them into specs a team can build. Or writes specs for simple, one-system features. |
| Weak (20-39) | Little spec work. More focused on testing, reports, or project support. |
| Gap (0-19) | No proof of spec work. |

### 2. Systems and Data Grasp (Weight: 25%)
Do they get how systems work well enough to ask the right things and spot gaps?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Maps end-to-end flows across many systems. Gets data flows, links, and tie-in points. Uses SQL or other tools to check data on their own. Can trace a problem from business impact back to root cause in the system. |
| Solid (60-79) | Good grasp of the systems they use. Can read data models and get basic tie-ins. Does some data checks. |
| Partial (40-59) | Works with systems at the top level. Gets inputs and outputs but not the guts. Leans on tech teams for data questions. |
| Weak (20-39) | Little systems know-how. Mainly focused on business flow, not system actions. |
| Gap (0-19) | No tech or systems contact. |

### 3. Working with People (Weight: 20%)
Can they run the room? BAs live where business meets tech. Both sides need to trust them.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Runs sessions with business and tech people. Talks both languages with ease. Manages clashing goals. Gets sign-off on tough calls. |
| Solid (60-79) | Talks well with both business and tech teams. Can run spec sessions. Handles normal team dynamics. |
| Partial (40-59) | Good with one side (business or tech) but weaker with the other. Or works mainly with one group. |
| Weak (20-39) | Mostly takes orders rather than leading talks. Little multi-group work. |
| Gap (0-19) | No proof of work with stakeholders. |

### 4. Domain Know-How (Weight: 15%)
Do they know the field well enough to ask smart questions and catch what others miss?

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Deep field knowledge in the right area (finance, health, insurance, etc.). Gets the rules, business flows, and field-specific terms. Can push back on business people from a place of knowledge. |
| Solid (60-79) | Good working knowledge of the field. Knows key flows and terms. |
| Partial (40-59) | General business sense that transfers. New to this field but has learned others before. |
| Weak (20-39) | No fitting field skill. Would need a lot of ramp time. |
| Gap (0-19) | No business field knowledge. |

Note: domain weight goes up when the JD calls out field skill (e.g., "finance experience required"). In those cases, raise to 25% and lower Requirements Work to 20%.

### 5. Process and Method (Weight: 10%)
Do they get how delivery works? Agile, waterfall, hybrid. Not just the meeting names, but how to make the process run.

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Strong (80-100) | Has worked in many delivery styles. Knows when to use what. Has made team processes better. Can manage backlogs, run grooming sessions, and plan across sprints or releases. |
| Solid (60-79) | At ease in agile or waterfall setups. Gets backlog care and sprint rhythm. |
| Partial (40-59) | Has worked within a method but did not drive it. Follows the process without shaping it. |
| Weak (20-39) | Little grasp of delivery methods. |
| Gap (0-19) | No process work. |

## Seniority Calibration

| Level | Scope Expected |
|---|---|
| Junior BA (0-2 years) | One feature or process. Needs help with spec format. Still learning to ask good questions. |
| Mid BA (2-5 years) | Owns specs for a workstream or project. Runs sessions alone. Handles mid-level depth. |
| Senior BA (5-8 years) | Owns specs across many workstreams. Handles complex, multi-system problems. Teaches junior BAs. Trusted by both business and tech. |
| Lead BA (8+ years) | Shapes the BA practice. Handles the hardest, most vague problems. Shapes the delivery approach and team setup. Often plays a hybrid BA/PM role. |

## BA-to-PM Transition Signals

Many BA roles overlap with PM work. Look for these signs that a BA was acting as a de facto PM:

- Owned what to build next (not just writing down what others chose)
- Set a product vision or plan for their area
- Made tradeoff calls between scope, timeline, and quality
- Owned the backlog with power to say "no"
- Tracked results after launch, not just specs before launch

If these signs are present, the person may score well on PM criteria too. Flag this in the Adjacent Work section.

## Common Scoring Traps

- Don't assume all BAs are the same. A BSA at a bank doing rule-based reports lives in a different world than a BA at a startup writing user stories.
- "Gathering requirements" sounds passive. Look for proof of spec WORK, which means study, challenge, and blend, not just note-taking.
- SQL skills are a strong plus. A BA who can check data on their own is far better than one who files tickets for every question.
- Certs (CBAP, CCBA) are neutral signals. They don't hurt but they don't predict results. Weight real work over credentials.
